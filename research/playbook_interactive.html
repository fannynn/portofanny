AI Visibility SEO B2B SaaS Content Production Research-Driven

Core Position

The useful shift is not “how do I rank one more page?” It is “how do I become easy for AI systems to understand, cite, and recommend when buyers ask specific questions?”

What matters now

Deal breakers, entity clarity, comparison content, proof assets, and representation quality.

What dies first

Generic top-of-funnel filler, vague positioning, and dashboards pretending noisy AI data is clean truth.

Main bias of the playbook

It is intentionally harsher on generic informational content than many SEO teams will be comfortable with.

Operational Workflow

  1. Map buyer deal breakers, follow-up questions, and comparison prompts before touching keyword expansion.
  2. Fix entity clarity before scaling content. If the company is fuzzy, more content just creates more fuzzy material.
  3. Write pages that survive chunking and citation. Important sections should still work when extracted from the page.
  4. Prioritize comparison pages, alternatives pages, integration pages, and proof pages over generic awareness filler.
  5. Build briefs from mined inputs like reviews, objections, support docs, and winning competitor patterns instead of starting from blank prompts.
  6. Diversify beyond your own site with review platforms, video, podcasts, and stronger third-party mentions.
  7. Measure mention rate, citation rate, recommendation rate, and representation quality separately.

If a team still treats AI visibility as a future problem, it is already late.

Useful Examples

Bad chunk

Our CRM platform empowers modern teams with flexible workflows, seamless collaboration, and innovative automation for growing businesses.

Too vague. It sounds polished but gives neither buyers nor machines anything concrete to work with.

Better chunk

This CRM is built for agencies with 10-100 staff that need QuickBooks sync, client-level permissions, and pipeline reporting without Salesforce-level setup time.

Specific audience, real use case, and clear selection criteria. That is much easier to retrieve and recommend.

Better comparison-page outline
  1. Direct verdict and best-fit summary
  2. Who each product is for
  3. Pricing differences
  4. Integration differences
  5. Onboarding friction
  6. Deal breakers
  7. Recommendation by scenario

Measurement That Is Not Fake

“Rank tracking but with AI screenshots” is not enough. It is brittle, easy to overread, and usually too shallow.

Mention rate

How often the brand appears at all across a prompt set.

Citation rate

How often a page or domain is used as a source.

Recommendation rate

How often the brand is framed as a fit rather than just named.

Representation audit

Whether the system describes pricing, ICP, strengths, and weaknesses correctly.

Judgment Calls

What this playbook rejects
  • Schema-only thinking as a serious AI visibility strategy.
  • Generic top-of-funnel production as the default growth plan.
  • Blind trust in new AI reporting layers that still group messy data together.
Where it takes a side
  • AI visibility is not just old SEO with a fresh label.
  • Teams should invest in AI-sensitive commercial content now, not later.
  • AI-generated filler can work tactically, but it is a weak long-term moat.
Who to follow cautiously

Metehan Yesilyurt is interesting, but some of his most memorable ideas feel more experimental than validated. Useful for edge-case inspiration, not the safest foundation for a team operating system.
